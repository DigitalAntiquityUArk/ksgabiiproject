Roman Burial Practices

Roman burials ranged from simple to elaborate, but both often included some sort of ceramics or grave goods, which were buried along with the body as a sign of wealth. "The simplest tombs of the Roman world were holes in the ground," without markers, though there was often an amphora in which offerings could be poured.1

Cappucina tombs are so called because of their roof, made of rooftiles called tegula. They are also known as 'fossa' graves, but regardless of terminology are one of the more common forms of tomb, not requiring much wealth, and still creating adequate protection around the body.2

Sarcophagae are uncommon in the Roman world, but when they were used they were generally made of wood. However, some examples of lead coffins, like the one at Gabii, do exist. Thermoanalysis and endoscopy can provide answers as to who was buried in this style, and why.3

Infant Burials

The burial of children depended on the age at which the child died. Infants under 4 months were often buried unceremoniously; over 4 months, and the body may have been placed in a pair of amphorae and given a small burial. The older the child, the more elaborate the burial, but unlike adults, children under five were almost always buried within city walls.4

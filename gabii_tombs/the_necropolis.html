The Necropolis at Area B

The 'necropolis' of Gabii is a bit of a misleading name—in the Roman world, a necropolis was a formal cemetery, a 'city of the dead,' but Gabii is different. Though there are many graves present, they are found within the city walls, amongst the living. Since Roman Law stated against this, it is clear that Gabii, an ally of Rome, was not following Roman laws at the time of these burials. At Gabii the Necropoleis "seem, so far as can be determined by accepted criteria, to disappear at the close of the sixth century BCE."1

Some of these make sense—there are a number of infant burials present in Area B. Children were often given simple burials within city walls; the older the child, the more elaborate, such as the double-amphora in SU 1349.

The grave goods in the Gabii Necropolis include metals—metal pins, metal rings, and entirely lead sarcophagus. This points towards the burials being before the 4th century BCE, as contemporaneous necropolei in Rome itself show this trend. For example, cappuccina tomb #34 (SU 1342) contains iron items, so it is likely not a late tomb (fourth-third-second century BCE), if Gabii is following Roman burial customs. The ark dates that cappuccina between -900 to +800 (nearly 1000 years, hardly so before -400, which fits with the information we know about Gabii being 6th c BCE, some 5th

The ceramics found in Gabii vary, but they are mostly the types of pottery used for cooking and eating (internal red slip, commonware). Commonware is found in much larger numbers than other types of ceramic—cappuccina #34 contains 185 sherds of commonware; the second most numerous ceramic is amphora, with 33 sherds.

Ryberg, Inez Scott. 1937. “The Esquiline Necropolis in the Fifth Century B. C.”. American Journal of Archaeology 41 (1). Archaeological Institute of America: 100–106.

The Tombs

SU 1046: With only a handful of similar examples known, the 1000 lb lead coffin unearthed at Gabii in 2009 is a bit of a mystery. According to Nicola Terrenato, the occupant was likely an ordinary Roman of an elevated social rank—a sarcophagus entirely of lead representing a large amount of expendable wealth in 2-4th century Italy. The coffin is oriented North-South, and contained no other grave goods.1

SU 1008: SU 1008 is part of a well-preserved grave, oriented East-West. Rather than a simple hole, the grave is lined with mortared stone. That, and the abundance of ceramics found , suggest that the citizen buried was probably decently middle-class, wealthy enough to afford a grave that would last. However, the grave's proximity to a plough field also suggest that some of those ceramics may be later additions, caused by agricultural disturbances of the earth. With a date range from 500 BC - 300 AD, it is difficult to be certain.

SU 1349: This East-West SU encompasses two commonware amphorae, but what they hold is not wine—the jars contain the remains of an infant. This was not an uncommon practice, and this infant burial from around the first century BCE is a standard example. The child was likely 4-6 months old, as younger tend to have less elaborate burials.2

SU 1123: Adjacent to a wall in the Southwest corner of Area B is a cappucina tomb, covered by tegula rooftiles. A simple tomb from around the first century BCE, and Oriented East-West, the grave fill finds include a button and a game piece.

SU 1026: In the south-central part of Area B, oriented North-South, lies a grave containing a truncated skeleton, its feet missing, with included ceramics indicating a burial from 900 BCE to 180 AD. This is the simplest of Roman-style burials, with tegula rooftiles covering the body, and a few grave goods. Buried with the body were a figurine and a ring of a non-precious metal, likely iron, but much deteriorated over time.
